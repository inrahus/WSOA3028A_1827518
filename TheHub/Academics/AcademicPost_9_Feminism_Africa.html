The Importance of Feminism In Africa and African Internet.

Technology, and the internet in particular, has long been a medium that shapes reality. It is something with the ability to change the very way people interact and communicate - how they see the world and how they are seen in it as well.

For the longest time, the issue of inequal access to the internet has created a platform that is rapidly replicating the supressive reality of the outerweb of today. The internet's ability to be manipulated allows the opression of the African voices and history to continue when it can be used in the opposite effect to allow the introduction of genuine visibilty of the African context and it's diverse nature.

In the article published by Sheena Magenya, there is an exploration of how the online space has been used and adapted to further increase and aid the attack on women of colour, and the LGBTQ+ community and the gender non-comforming community. Magyenya explains that the bridging of technolgy with reality has created a space where there is no offline processes to deal with the happenings and doings that occur in the online space - the lack of legislation in African countries reagrding this have shaped the online space as a "safe"space for consequence-less violence that targets these minorities.

Feminism in the African context has never been easy. With many cultures stemming from a matriarchal hierarchy, many Africans believe there to be a sense of "Un-Africanness" associated with being a feminist. The President of the African National Women's Organisation, Yejide Orunmila, agrees. Orunmila argues that the ideals of fenimism higly contradict the African natural inclination of struggling together. Orunmila states that it creates a divide among men and women of the continent, changing the fight form that of the colonial opression and capitalist system, to a fight that seeks a sense of validation from the primary, colonial oppressors - who, in fact, where the ones who, with the introduction of slavery, shifted the status of African women from positions of power to positions where they were exploited and the patriarchy was favoured.

In comparison however, when met with the same question of feminism being "un-African", Minna Salami, disagrees. Salami acknowledged the issues to do with the colonial powers, and how they have affected the exploitation of women in the African context, but states that comtemporary feminism highlights that the gender roles we find ourselves in are not biological conditions that determine how we behave socially, but rather that we are made to socialise to fulfil said gender roles. The award winning writer furthers her arguement and staes that pre colonial times were not exclusionary of the patriarchal view, and that the current, presnt day patriarchy is responsible for the unrivaled suffering of the female in the African context.

The reason that feminism finds itself necessary in the African context, as Minna Salami emphasises, it is more un-African to allow and ignore the current inhumane discriminations of the female in Africa - and thereby also the non-binary minority and the LGBTQ+ communities struggles of similar note as well. By not acknowledging the presence and Africanness of Feminism, we ignore the erasure of African narrative by the very colonisers who systematically opressed and continue to oppress us with the evolution of society on the digital platforms. This is why there is such a necessity for the African Feminist on the internet - namely the African internet.

As Magenya explains, African Feminists need to take up space on the internet, tell their stroies, th stories of their people, their countries, their cultures - give the persepctive tha has long been ommitted from history, and open up the digital platform for more African women, queers and non-binaries to do the same. With the current state of the world, especially with the current quarantine and the higher chance of radicalisation of ideas through the web because of it - it is most important to use this opportunity to denounce the oppressive reality to manifest and overtake the safe space initally created to educate, communicate and relate. With the beginning of the discourses surrounding the idea of feminism in the African context - and the acknowledgement of both its shortcomings to the context and necessary issues it addresses, it is clear that there must be a claim and representation of feminism and what its ideas represent in the African context compared to both that of the East and the West. Feminism finds itself a global movement, an international form of resistance to the inequalities. And as Salami notes, Africa remains a revolutionary resistance in the world today, and so Feminism is no doubt something very African.

Based on information from The Making a Feminist Internet in Africa: Why The Internet Need African Feminists and Feminism by Sheena Magenya and The Question: Is Feminism Un-African? by African Report.

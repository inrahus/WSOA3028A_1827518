#5, The First Browser Wars.

12 March 2020

Prior to reading this post, I do suggest you take a look at The Mosaic War - as it defines a majority of terms that may be used below, and also gives context to Netscape's origins.

After the Mosaic War, Netscape began to gain traction with the constant development of new features to their browser. Around the time that they were reaching a stable peak, Microsoft and Bill Gates himself, realised the profits that could be gained in the web browser industry. Prior to Netscape's great overtake on the markets, Microsoft had not realised that although they were able to supply home software, they were losing profits because web browsers were sold seperately from operating systems.

Microsoft first took steps to communicate with Netscape in hopes of merging together and furthering Microsoft's future in web development. However, talks did not end well and eventually Internet Explorer was released by Microsoft. Netscape did not take the release seriously as it did not present an initial threat to the highly developed Netscape Navigator. That was until Bill Gates announced plans of Web Domination on the 7th of December, 1995, and the First Browser War began.

Within the following years, Microsoft and Netscape challnged each other, each making minor iterations to their browser with each release, and the other responding with equal or more iterations with theirs. Iterations were released monthly and eventually, as the final 3.0 versions of each browser were released. The standings between the teo were realised on the markets. With Netscape dominating the web browser usage at a major 90%.

However, on the 7th of October, 1997 - the tables turned. Microsoft released Internet Explorer 4.0. A web broswer - the first web broswer, to be intergrated into the operating system. People no longer had to seperately purchase web browsers as Microsoft now supplied the buyers of their operating system, with a web browser included.

Microsoft owned 90% of the market when it came to operating systems. By releasing the browser alongside their system, they saw a steady takeover of the web browser market. And by their 5.0 version release of Internet Explorer, they owned 60% of the market.

Netscape never recovered from the losses and in when Internet Explorer 6.0 was released in 2001, the First Browser Wars ended. Microsoft theunderdog winner, and Netscape dissolved. Based on information from The History of the Web: Browser Wars and acodez: The History of the Browser Wars

Information Geography.

Information has the ability to not only reflect but it is something that also shapes the way we see and understand different parts of the world. And so, because of it's nature, information geographies has become a political playground. Information geography works on the basis that information is from, about and transforms and evolves somewhere. A means used by collectives to define and redefine the narratives of the different parts of the world. These geographies are influenced by a number of uneven geographic factors - namely: access, participation and representation.

Information has always varied - in terms of it's roots, by who and how it is absorbed and how it is contextualised in terms of it's relation to geography. It is because of this flexible nature of information, that it has become intricately tied into power dynamics. The way that the information is represented can create shared ideals, understandings and views. And so, these augmentations become a part of the place. The information no longer alone reflect the place, but it creates annotations - associations. Simply put, information geography is a crucial form of control and power - one that is heavily intergrated into the media that we consume information from, it always has been.

Prior to digital geographies of information, information was far few between. It was often hard to find because it was contained to one particular geographic area. On top of that, information was inaccessible because of academic nature - few understood the available information, and even fewer could conribute to it. Since pre-digital forms were bound to their material medium, they could never transend geographical barriers and thus gave way to a hegamoic representation of the world. This supremist takeover of information was a great factor in the power relations - influencing not only politics, but the socio-ecomomic environments that thet were present in. These tradition mediums - mostly books, or paper formats - are distinguishable by their geographic inconsistencies. Most of the time information was produced and spread among the Northern regions of the globe. The Southern hemisphere played a significantly minor role in the establishment and dispersion of the information. This uneven relationship gave way to a a marginalised and exclusive view on information.

With the introduction of digital mediums however, the barriers that were previously very prevalent, have lowered. Digital forms created a more broad based sense of participation and representation. It allowed for people to play a more key role in the augmentations and shaping of the imformation geographies. This technology has the ability to equalise opportunities and allow people to - not only have access to, but also contribute to the content regardless of geographic placement. But, just because it has these capabilites, does not mean that it is being used to fulfil it's full potential. Theoretically, this platform should allow participation of diffrent representations and geographies, but because of access, participation and representation geographies, the full extent of the digital platform is limited.

Geographies of Access.
Although there are several factors, such as internet penetration and overall population access, that play a role in the accessibility of digital mediums - the most prevalent and still ongoing issue is that of broadband affordabilty. The relative costs of fixed broadband subscription charges is one of the major setbacks when it omes to access - i.e. the cost of access where there is access available. In Europe and North America, the cost of subscription is relatively low in relation to the avergae salary - costing what would require a few hours of a month's work to afford - approximately 10% or less of the total income for the month.

In Africa, however, the cost can vary from a minimum of 10% of the total salary, to a surplus of 250% of the average income of the country. Costs remain a pivotal barrier in the African continent and can majorly be contributed not just to the exploitation of the everyday civilian by these few major corporations who wish to capatilise on the access that is necessary for the digital growth of the country - but also the fact that the incomes in the African countries is simply, too low.

Research suggest sand shows that even though prices have in many places across the continent, dropped significantly - it is still an unreasomable feat to consider it as a commodity when that same amount could eaily contribute to the necessities that the average civilian requires their salary to cover. It is a matter of importance and the hierarchy of needs that is not only heavily present in out continent but in other developing countries worldwide.

Geographies of Participation.
When it comes to this particluar contributing factors, Africa finds itself a victim of the very power play that information geography aims to control. Because of the lack of access to the internet - which can be seen in the Western dominated domain names and lack of presence in the GitHub collaborative community. Africa finds itself close to complete ommittance in a digital space that is heavily dominated by the Noth American and European contributors to the internet.

Another way that participation was checked was through the local editings of pages - which were relevant in context - through Wikipedia.Although Kenya and Niigeria have the most local contributions to content, the rest of the continent contributes only 5% local content which is stark in contrast to that of the Americas and Europe, who's local content and contributions is over 75% each.

The lack of access to technology finds Africa participating in involuntary ommitance from digital spaces and thereby furthering the inability to affect their geographical information. It allows for the views of the other to be more prominent, and thus presents the same issues as the predigital era - the marginalisation and exclusive view of the geography.

The Impact of Inequality on the Digital Divide.

The Digital Divide is greatest manifestation of Digital Inequality.

This form of inequality had not only become as prominent as traditional forms of inequality - which include: race, class, gender etc - but it has become intwined with the tradtional forms, as mentioned by the authors of "Digital inequalites and why they matter". This divide plays a major role in several aspects of life. With the merging of the digital platforms and reality, the actions taken on either and the repercussions on both, the impact of the Digital Divide has allowed the world to indulge in a concept of technology dependency and insuffiency on several levels.

In the article by Carmen Steele, on the Digital Divide Council site, there is an exploration of several sectors that the Digital Divide has a large play in. Namely - as I will elaborate on - education, social interaction and society as a whole, and culture. Steele explains how income and digital literacy are crucial contributors to the Digital Divide, but also that these do not reflect the only factors that contribute to the racial and ethnic imbalance of digital platforms today. In the article, "Digital inequalities and why they matter", the authors identified that race, ethinicity and gender - regardless of the country's high level of technological adoption - present equal - if not additional disadvantage when it comes to the Digital Divide.The sectors that Steele elaborates on further display just how the disparities show themselves.

Although the internet serves as a rich accumlation of the world's knowledge, the lack of access to the internet itself already presents a fundamentally large gap - with no access to the internet, there is no access to it's knowledge. Without access to the knowledge of the how and what of the internet, there is no way to access it or further obtain knowlegde regarding it. Paired with this, the lack of technological literacy finds effect in the education sector, and the biggest victims of this, are countries with weak/new education systems - mainly in developing countries where the overall idea of technology is not as adept as that of the world that is comfortable with the medium. It firthers the disparity between not only the priveleged and underprivileged, the first and developing world countries, but also between generations in these seperate contexts. There is a divide between the people of the same generation because of the lack of access and lack of technological litaracy that is present to the other. Another factor that is affected by this controlled knowledge, is the gender factor, which is present in when it comes to both education and job opportunity.

Studies presented in the "Digital inequalites and why they matter" showed that women were spent less time using the access they had - if they did, and report less intensity of their time spent there, as well as having less activities to perform using this medium. These directly intersect with two main factors pointed out in the work - one, the gendering of technical skills and content, and two - which stems from this - is the gendering of jobs and opportunities. The internet has long stood as an extension of the reality we live in, and thereby serves to extend not only our interests but the roles and expctations that we are socialised into. It is why many women spent less time investing into the internet - unless mostly for the social aspect that was also required and forced on them outside the digital platforms they occupied. This furthers the Digital Divide between men and women, showing how the digital space is in need of a bridging - by opening and allowing more space for the feminine to explore aspects of the digital.

Another factor that majorly contributes, is that of race, ethnicity and culture. As Steele mentions in her article - and as seen previously when discussing education - the limit of access to the internet has left developing countries behind, furthering the gap as they are still beginning to incorporate technology into their existing culture. With the countries that are more developed, we see now technology and access to technology has become a commidity and deeply intergrated in many aspects of the cultures present there today. However, even in these developed countries race and ethnicity have proven to be major setbacks in terms of access to digital platforms and thus contributors to the Divide.

In the article, "Digital inequalites and why they matter", the evidence displays how the minority of users are people of colour (POC). POCs experience a mass of inequalities outside the digital space and it can be seen in hw it translates into the digital. The lack of access, different in class with reference to race, ethinicity and gender show how the inequalities of race and ethnicity carry oeveronto the internet. As a majority of the population that has lack of access to the internet are POCs, we see how there is already a clear racial and ethnical divide. However, even in countries with access, POCs form minorities in terms of population as a whole and even in terms of income - how they are the general less earning, we see how these external factors further the Digital Divide in terms of ethnicity and race.

This as a whole represents exactly how we cannot seperate the idea of traditional discrimation from that of the digtal, as it is the tradition forms that influence the accessbility and ability to participate and represent on the digital platform, and thereby are forms of digital inequality. The effects of this on education, gender, race and culture in reality shows how the digital serves as an extention of said reality, and how in return, these factors contribute to the Digital Divide.

Based on information from The Impacts of the Digital Divide by Carmen Steele and Digital inequalities and why they matter by Robinson et al.

Racism and Technology.

In the podcast, Radical AI - the eighth episode features Ruha Benjamin, an accomplishd author who discusses the relations between the dimensions of science, technology and medicine, focusing on the relationship between inequity, justice, knowledge and power. In the episode that features her, Benjamin elaborates on the relation - and necessity for awareness, of the socio-political within technology centred spaces.

Through the discussion, Benjamin relays that the way we learnt to define racism, versus what racism actually is, is an important distinction to make. Benjamin further explains that although there isn't an intent of malice behind the structures, developments and laws in place - there is still effect that is had, which is in turn racially discriminatory. She exemplifies the issue of drug, and drug enforcement penalties whcih affect racial groups in the American context severely differently. The severity allowed by the penalties of the drugs - which are reagrdless, more porminent in black societies of America - are a way in which the effect of the law, is racist. She goes on the apply a similar example in terms of technology and it's advances.

In the context of soap dispensers, Benjamin showcases how the near infrared technology used, is discrimatory. Alhough the technology was developed with good intent, it cannot be denied that because of the nauture of the technology - i.e. infrared technology, would only allow the soap to dispense onto lighter skin tones becasue darker skin tones, with more melanin, woud absorb more ligh instead of bounce it back and triggering the release of soap. Benjamin further goes on to show how it is in the systems like this, that reflect the skewness of the data input and the lack of the peope who could address it in the process of creation.

Benjamin clearly notes that in the circumstances where it is most necessary for representation, there is a lack of it. The racism that we are taught exists, but this unintentional divide is furthered by the gap present in the developmental stages of these technologies.

So how do we fix it?

The admittance and realisation thta these divides exist and are a problem, is a necessary step into dealing with the problem - you have to acknowledge the lack of POC and particularly black presence in these spaces, as a problem.

You also need to individually recognise how is it that you are contributing to the problem and how it is you plan to tackle thise individual biases you have. It is the responsibility of those in the fields, who occupy the spaces ad positions of power, to be self concious and aware of their prejudices and how these affect the approach and how the process needs to be altered so that the results cater to all.

It is in the moment that we use our innocence as an excuse for our ignorance, that we have accepted the systems that are desgined to further emphasis the racial divide between us.

As a POC who has herself experienced - and been at the end of a discriminatory ssytem - and used a system with such a bias to her advantage, I believe that is is important, in the context that we are individually, to acknowledge the biases we have, and that the systems we fact and create will have, so that we can lessen the recial divide in technology. There needs to be an allowance of vulnerability and an acceptance of one's wrongdoing, and a release of pride, for people to understand and acknowledge the systems that simultaneously benifit them, at the cost of the other.

There needs to be a sense of active learning abou thtese processes - and as Ruda Benjamin meentioned, this means you can't just go around calling everything racist - you need to be able to understand and see why it is something with inherently racism effects - and acknowlegde that there is sometimes more than just a removal of the system necessary - sometimes the removal of the systemic barrier requires a reformation or revisitation of the technology and sources that it is based on.

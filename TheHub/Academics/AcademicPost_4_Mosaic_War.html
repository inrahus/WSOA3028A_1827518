#4, The Mosaic War (Prelude to the Browser Wars)

9 March 2020

Briefly described, the Browser Wars refers to an era in which Microsoft and Netscape fought for power in order to determine who would be the one to dominate the market. This period of rapid development defined the browsers we so easily access today.

The first browser WorldWideWeb was created by Tim Burner-Lee. After he developed this technology, several other HTML readers based on his creation began to pop up. It wasn't until students from the NCSA (National Center for Supercomputing Applications) at the University of Illinois developed "Mosaic", that the idea of what a browser was, changed completely. "Mosaic" included several accessibiliy enhancing features that defined it in the public space. However, after a fallout with the University and several legal issues, the founders of Mosaic Communications Corporation, whicj held "Mosaic" - Andreessen and Jim Clark - renamed the company, Netscape, and the product they offered, based on "Mosaic" that Andreessen had co-developed, was now known as the "Netscape Navigator". Leaving "Mosaic" to the University of Illinois.

The older, "Mosiac" and the new "Netscape Navigator" competed on the market and the short, sweet Mosaic War ended with "Netscape Navigator" dominating the markets and public opinion. Netscape acheived this through cutting down the web page loading time, offering a free inclusive email service, as well as still having all the features that were loved from "Mosaic".

For years Netscape thrived with it's product and the continuous additions of features that prioritised convinience, many of which are still influential today.

Based on information from The History of the Web: Browser Wars and acodez: The History of the Browser Wars
